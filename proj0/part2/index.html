Part 2: Architectural Perspective Compression

Repeat the selfie experiment in reverse for an urban scene to reveal perspective “compression.”

Assignment:

Pick a view down a long street (or a campus path). First, zoom in from far away and take a photo. Then, walk forward in the direction of your first shot and take a second photo without zoom so the scene appears approximately the same size in both images. The first picture should look more flattened/compressed than the second. Think about why this happens.

Results

Method

  1. Stand far back and frame a long street or pathway. Take Shot A by zooming in.
  2. Walk forward toward the scene and take Shot B with no zoom, matching the apparent size of the scene.
  3. Keep the camera level and at a consistent height to isolate the perspective effect.
  4. Compare spacing between distant objects, the convergence of lines, and the sense of depth.

Why This Works (Short Explanation)

Perspective depends on camera position (distance), not focal length by itself. When you stand far away, the relative distances to near vs. far objects are more similar, so the background looks closer to the foreground—this is perceived as “compression.” Moving closer exaggerates the relative distance differences, increasing depth and line convergence. Zoom just changes framing.

  • Framing roughly matched between the two shots.
  • A brief reflection comparing “compression” vs. “depth” and explaining why.
